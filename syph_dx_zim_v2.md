# **Abstract**

**Background:** Syphilis diagnosis in resource-limited settings relies on syndromic management and treponemal-based antibody screening tests that cannot distinguish active infection from past treated infection, both of which can lead to overtreatment. Point-of-care (POC) tests that detect active infection could reduce unnecessary treatment.

**Methods:** We developed an agent-based model of co-transmitting syphilis and HIV, calibrated to Zimbabwe using population-based survey data (ZIMPHIA) and UNAIDS estimates. We modeled five scenarios involving two hypothetical new point-of-care (POC) diagnostic products introduced from 2027: (1) a test detecting *T pallidum* DNA in genital ulcer specimens, and (2) a non-treponemal test identifying active infection following a treponemal-positive result, applied across antenatal care, key population, and HIV care settings. We also conducted a threshold-based cost analysis to assess whether reductions in unnecessary treatment alone could be cost-saving.

**Results:** Under the current standard of care, approximately 112,000 adult syphilis treatments are administered annually across all modeled detection and screening use cases, of which \~80% are unnecessary (i.e. administered to individuals who do not have an active syphilis infection). Combining both diagnostics reduces overtreatment to 24% (a 3.4-fold reduction). Across use cases, each test avoids approximately 0.4–0.8 unnecessary treatments, implying cost-savings whenever the test price is less than 40–80% of the treatment cost.

**Conclusions:** Even under the conservative assumption that current syndromic management and screening practices achieve high syphilis treatment rates among care-seeking individuals, introducing POC diagnostics for active syphilis could substantially reduce overtreatment while maintaining treatment of individuals with active infection. Reductions in unnecessary treatment alone may be cost-saving at low test prices, even without accounting for downstream health outcomes. 

# **Introduction**

The global burden of syphilis, a bacterial sexually transmitted infection (STI), is both sizable and increasing. The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates approximately  8 million adults were newly infected with syphilis in 2022 (1). In addition, nearly 700,000 cases are transmitted from pregnant women to their babies annually (1). These congenital syphilis cases make up essentially all of the estimated mortality and morbidity burden of syphilis and approximately 85% of the overall global disability-adjusted life year (DALY) burden of all STIs (2). Much of this burden results from persistent challenges in the accurate and timely diagnosis and management of syphilis (3). Management of syphilis in resource-limited settings currently relies on a combination of syndromic management of genital ulcer disease (GUD) and screening for pregnant women and key populations (KP). While these strategies improve access to treatment, they result in substantial overtreatment and undertreatment for those who don’t meet narrow screening criteria. 

Syndromic management of GUD, recommended by WHO for settings without laboratory capacity, treats all patients presenting with genital ulcers presumptively for multiple pathogens including *T pallidum.* When implemented as per the guidelines, this approach has sensitivity of 100% but specificity of 0%, meaning that many patients without syphilis receive unnecessary treatment (1). Despite this low specificity, syndromic management remains recommended due to the severe health consequences of untreated syphilis and the lack of viable alternatives. Clinical characteristics of ulcers have poor predictive value for ulcer aetiology (3) and serological rapid diagnostic tests cannot reliably be used in patients presenting with GUD, as early primary syphilis may precede seroconversion (aka “window period”). Studies across sub-Saharan Africa have documented that syphilis accounts for only 10-30% (4–7) of GUD cases in many settings, with herpes simplex virus being the predominant cause, yet all patients receive anti-syphilitic therapy under syndromic management protocols. In practice, sensitivity may be lower than 100%, resulting in missed cases, as clinicians may deviate from guidelines by treating selectively based on ulcer appearance or by using serological rapid treponemal diagnostic tests despite the window period. 

Similarly, screening programs for pregnant women and key populations (KP) face diagnostic limitations. The adoption of single treponemal and dual HIV/treponemal POC tests in antenatal care (ANC) has improved screening coverage for syphilis in low resource settings. However, most point-of-care syphilis tests are treponemal antibody-based assays that cannot distinguish between active infection requiring treatment and previously treated and resolved infections. For individuals at high-risk who undergo repeated screening over time, the combination of more frequent screening and higher prevalence may lead to repeated unnecessary treatments. Unneeded use of benzathine penicillin for these cases can contribute to stock-outs, which have been a significant global concern over the past 5-10 years and can lead to undertreatment (8–10).

The development of novel point-of-care diagnostics offers potential solutions to reduce this overtreatment burden. For symptomatic presentations, point-of-care tests capable of detecting the presence of treponemes in genital ulcers could improve the specificity of GUD management. For screening in antenatal care, key populations, and people living with HIV, POC tests that confirm active infection following a positive treponemal screening test could substantially reduce unnecessary treatment.

Zimbabwe, with estimated active syphilis prevalence of 0.9% among all adults and 2.9% among adults living with HIV (11–13), and a generalized HIV epidemic (11.8% prevalence among adults aged 15–49 in 2020), represents an important setting for evaluating the potential impact of improved diagnostic approaches. In this study, we use STIsim, an agent-based model of co-transmitting STIs, to estimate the extent of overtreatment under current diagnostic algorithms and quantify the potential reduction in overtreatment achievable with POC tests for active infection across both symptomatic presentations and key screening settings. We focus specifically on overtreatment and do not model downstream health outcomes or reductions in undertreatment; instead, we assess whether new diagnostics may provide value even under the simplifying assumption that current approaches have good sensitivity for detecting syphilis among care-seeking individuals.

# **Methods**

## **Model overview**

We used STIsim, an agent-based model of co-transmitting sexually transmitted infections (STIs) built on the Starsim disease modeling platform (14,15). We initialized a population of 10,000 agents representative of Zimbabwe in 1985, scaled to the national population using UN estimates of age-specific fertility, age- and sex-disaggregated mortality, and overall population size (16). We created an age- and risk-structured sexual network with three risk groups (low, medium, and high) governing partnership formation rates and concurrency. We modeled participation in transactional sex by assigning approximately 10% of women to a high-risk group, within which 10% engage in sex work at any given time, corresponding to an estimated \~40,000 female sex workers nationally (17). Pregnancy timing and frequency are governed by UN World Population Prospects age-specific fertility rates for Zimbabwe, which determine each woman's probability of conception at each time step. Pregnant women reduce sexual risk behavior, reverting to their pre-pregnancy risk profiles postpartum.

## **Disease models**

We modeled HIV transmission, progression, diagnosis, and treatment as previously described (14). Briefly, HIV-infected individuals progress through acute, chronic, and AIDS stages with stage-specific infectiousness. We modeled the scale-up of ART in Zimbabwe from 2004 onwards, with ART reducing transmission probability by 96% among virally suppressed individuals. HIV testing, diagnosis, and ART initiation were modeled separately for sex workers (higher testing rates), individuals with low CD4 counts, and the general population.

We simulated syphilis (*T pallidum*) transmission and natural history. Natural history parameters were informed by published clinical guidelines and prior modeling studies (1,18–19). After acquisition, infections progress through an incubation period (mean 21 days), primary infection characterized by chancres (mean duration 6 weeks; visible in approximately 80% of cases in men, where chancres typically occur on external genitalia, and 30% in women, where chancres are more often internal and go unnoticed), secondary infection with disseminated rash (mean duration 3.6 months), and subsequent latency. The first 24 months of latency are classified as early latent (1); thereafter, infections transition to late latent. Upon entering latency, 35% of individuals are assigned to reactivate to secondary syphilis, with the timing of reactivation drawn from a lognormal distribution (mean 1 year, SD 1 year from entry to latency). The remaining 65% do not reactivate; of these, 35% eventually progress to tertiary syphilis (mean 20 years).

Sexual transmission probability is stage-dependent: primary syphilis is modeled as 5-fold more infectious than secondary, while latent infections have 10% of secondary-stage infectiousness that decays exponentially with a half-life of 6 months. This parameterization produces a transmission profile where approximately 60% of sexual transmissions originate from primary-stage infections, 30% from secondary, and the remainder from early latent, consistent with evidence that transmission is concentrated in early infectious stages and driven by undetected primary infections in networks with rapid partner turnover (20).

For maternal-to-child transmission (MTCT), birth outcomes among MTCT-affected pregnancies depend on the maternal stage (21). Among mothers with primary or secondary syphilis, 10% of pregnancies result in neonatal death, 20% in stillbirth, 45% in congenital syphilis, and 25% in normal outcomes. Early latent mothers produce somewhat better outcomes (5% neonatal death, 10% stillbirth, 40% congenital, 45% normal), while late latent mothers have predominantly normal outcomes (10% stillbirth, 10% congenital, 80% normal). When an infected pregnant woman is successfully treated, her fetus is also treated: fetal treatment efficacy is 98% if administered before the third trimester, declining to 75% thereafter, reflecting the reduced effectiveness of late treatment in preventing congenital syphilis. If fetal treatment is successful, adverse birth outcomes (stillbirth, neonatal death, congenital syphilis) are averted. The quality of ANC screening thus directly influences congenital syphilis outcomes, as earlier detection and treatment of maternal infection improves fetal treatment success.

We modeled bidirectional interactions between HIV and syphilis with the strength of association varying depending on an individual’s HIV treatment status (22-23). HIV infection increases susceptibility to syphilis acquisition (calibrated median relative susceptibility 2.5; Table S6), while active syphilis increases susceptibility to HIV acquisition (calibrated median relative susceptibility 1.9; Table S6).

## **Screening and diagnostic strategies**

We considered four entry points through which syphilis infections are currently detected and treated under the standard of care (Table 1). For GUD syndromic management, we assumed a sensitivity of 80% rather than the theoretical 100%, reflecting real-world deviations from guidelines such as clinicians treating selectively based on ulcer appearance or using treponemal POCs despite the window period. Because the treponemal-based screening tests used in the ANC, KP, and PLHIV pathways detect antibodies that persist after successful treatment, previously infected individuals who have cleared their infection will continue to test positive. This drives substantial overtreatment, particularly among key populations with high cumulative exposure. Care-seeking probability for the GUD syndromic pathway varies by sex, risk group, and year. For ANC screening, positive results lead to treatment of both the mother (with benzathine penicillin) and the fetus. Newborns of ANC-positive mothers are scheduled for clinical examination at delivery.

## **Intervention scenarios**

We evaluated five scenarios involving two novel diagnostic products introduced from 2027 onwards, compared with the current standard of care (Table 2). The first product is a POC test detecting *T pallidum* directly from genital ulcer specimens, replacing syndromic management for the GUD use case (assumed sensitivity 90% for primary syphilis and with 95% specificity). The second product is a POC non-treponemal (NT) active infection diagnostic used as a confirmatory step after a treponemal screen-positive result, reducing treatment of individuals with past rather than active infection (assumed sensitivity 90% and with 95% specificity). We evaluated the POC NT diagnostic separately for each of the three treponemal-screening use cases (ANC, KP, PLHIV) and in a combined scenario deploying both products across all use cases.

For key populations, we assumed low initial use of dual HIV/syphilis testing in 2027, with gradual replacement of single HIV tests over time, reaching approximately 50% of HIV testing encounters by 2040\. This was modeled as a scenario assumption, informed by WHO guidance supporting dual HIV/syphilis RDT use in ANC and other testing sites and by increasing use of rapid syphilis testing in key populations. This assumption is important because increasing uptake of dual HIV/syphilis screening among key populations would increase treatment triggered by treponemal positivity under the standard of care, and therefore increase overtreatment unless an active-infection test is added.

## **Model calibration**

We jointly calibrated HIV and syphilis parameters to Zimbabwe-specific epidemiological data using Bayesian optimization with the Optuna framework, implemented via the STIsim Calibration class. We fit 15 parameters simultaneously: 4 governing HIV transmission and treatment (transmission probability, condom efficacy, initial prevalence scaling, effective ART duration), 3 governing syphilis transmission (transmission probability, condom efficacy, relative primary-stage infectiousness), 3 governing sexual network structure (proportion of women and men in the lowest risk group, male mid-risk concurrency), 2 governing HIV-syphilis coinfection interactions (relative susceptibility in each direction), and 3 governing diagnostic testing rates (symptomatic care-seeking, ANC screening, and KP screening rate multipliers). Prior ranges and posterior estimates for all parameters are provided in Table S6.

Calibration targets included HIV prevalence among adults aged 15–49, people living with HIV, new HIV infections, HIV-related deaths, and ART coverage from UNAIDS estimates (2000–2024); active syphilis prevalence, active syphilis prevalence by sex, and active syphilis prevalence by HIV status from ZIMPHIA 2015-2016; and syphilis new infections and active case counts from Spectrum-STI estimates. We ran 2,000 Optuna trials. We applied higher calibration weights to syphilis-specific ZIMPHIA survey targets (weight 10–20) relative to HIV time series targets (weight 2–5), reflecting the greater precision and direct relevance of the cross-sectional survey data. 

## **Model analysis**

We selected the top 200 best-fitting parameter sets from the calibration and re-ran each as a single simulation to produce comprehensive epidemiological and treatment outcomes across all model outputs. We computed percentile statistics (median, 10th–90th percentiles) across the parameter sets, used for figures and uncertainty characterization. For the scenario analysis, we ran the top 10 best-fitting parameter sets forward from 1985 to 2041 under each of the five diagnostic scenarios, using a single random seed per parameter set. 

For each scenario, we computed the number of unnecessary treatments (which we also refer to as overtreatments), defined as treatments given to individuals without active syphilis (including those with serofast state from past infection and true false positives. We disaggregated outcomes by use case (GUD syndromic, ANC, KP, PLHIV). We report results as medians across calibrated parameter sets.

To characterize bottlenecks in the current standard of care, we constructed a care-seeking cascade for the GUD syndromic use case, estimating the proportion of new primary syphilis infections progressing through each step: developing a visible chancre (determined by the sex-weighted probability of symptomatic primary infection, approximately 51%), seeking care during the primary stage, testing positive under syndromic management (80%, reflecting imperfect adherence to guidelines as described above), and being correctly treated (98% treatment uptake).

We conducted a threshold-based cost analysis considering only testing costs and benzathine penicillin treatment costs. For each scenario, we calculated the number of unnecessary treatments avoided per test performed and used this to derive the break-even test-cost:treatment-cost ratio. We then expressed net cost impact as a function of this ratio.

All analyses were conducted using Python 3.11 with STIsim v1.5.2 and Starsim v3.3.0. Code is publicly available at [https://github.com/starsimhub/syph\_dx\_zim](https://github.com/starsimhub/syph_dx_zim).

# **Results**

## Model calibration to syphilis and HIV epidemiology

Figure 1 summarizes the calibrated model's fit to syphilis and HIV epidemiology in Zimbabwe. The model reproduces key epidemiological patterns across both diseases and is broadly consistent with survey data from the Zimbabwe Population-based HIV Impact Assessment (ZIMPHIA) surveys conducted in 2015-2016 and 2020\. Women engaged in transactional sex have substantially higher syphilis prevalence than the general female population across all age groups, consistent with their elevated exposure through higher partner turnover and concurrency (Figure 1A). Even so, we estimate that the prevalence of visible chancres that would present as GUD syphilis is low: among women engaged in transactional sex, an estimated 33% have been previously exposed to syphilis and would test positive on a treponemal RDT, 11% have a current untreated infection, 0.6% have primary-stage ulcerative disease, and just 0.2% have a visible chancre.

Figure 1B shows modeled HIV prevalence by age and sex across four time points: pre-ART (2005), 2016, 2020, and 2025 (projected). The model reproduces the characteristic age-sex pattern of HIV in Zimbabwe, with peak prevalence among women aged 30-35 and men aged 35-50. The epidemic shows a marked aging pattern over time: while prevalence among younger age groups has declined substantially between 2005 and 2025 (reflecting reduced incidence due to treatment-as-prevention and behavior change), prevalence among older adults has remained high or even increased, reflecting the success of ART in extending survival. The aging HIV epidemic has direct implications for syphilis-HIV coinfection dynamics, shown in Figure 1C. Our modeling analyses estimate that the ratio of syphilis prevalence among HIV-positive versus HIV-negative adults has been declining over time, from an estimated peak of approximately 14x in the early 2000s to around 5x by 2020\. Although HIV increases susceptibility to syphilis acquisition, the declining ratio reflects the fact that a growing share of HIV-positive individuals are older adults on ART who are less sexually active and therefore less exposed to syphilis.

For syphilis, women in transactional sex account for approximately 30% of new acquisitions and 36% of onward transmissions, while men engaged in transactional sex account for 20% of acquisitions and 38% of transmissions. This asymmetry reflects the epidemiological importance of male clients as a bridge population, who transmit syphilis acquired during transactional sex to their other sexual partners. A similar pattern is seen for HIV (Figure 1D).

We estimate that sexual transmission of syphilis is dominated by primary syphilis (55%), followed by secondary (43%) and early latent (2%) stages, reflecting the high infectiousness of early-stage disease (Figure 1E). Congenital transmission shows a different pattern: early latent syphilis contributes the largest share of adverse congenital outcomes (47%) followed by late latent (44%). Although per-pregnancy transmission risk is lower for late latent infections, the much larger pool of women with latent syphilis means more congenital  transmissions originate from this stage.

## Care-seeking cascades under the standard of care

The GUD syndromic cascade reveals that the vast majority of primary syphilis infections go undetected under current SOC (Figure 2). Of every 100 new primary syphilis infections, approximately half (51) develop a visible chancre, with visibility substantially lower in women (30%) than men (80%) due to the predominantly internal location of female chancres; the remainder occur at internal or painless sites that do not prompt clinical attention. Of those with visible ulcers, care-seeking probabilities in the model are 10% for men and 15–45% for women, with the highest rates among female sex workers, which implies that approximately one third (16/51) of individuals with visible chancres seek care during the primary window. Of those who do present, syndromic management correctly identifies 80% as requiring treatment. The net result is that approximately 12 in 100 primary syphilis infections are correctly treated through the GUD syndromic pathway.

## Treatment outcomes under the standard of care

GUD syndromic management accounts for approximately \~24,000 treatments per year but has the highest overtreatment rate: 88% of treatments through this pathway are given to individuals without active syphilis (Figure 3A–B). This reflects the fundamental limitation of syndromic management, which treats essentially all GUD presentations presumptively regardless of syphilis status. Because background GUD prevalence (\~1%) far exceeds the prevalence of syphilitic ulcers (\~0.1%, reflecting the small fraction of the population with primary syphilis who have visible chancres), most people presenting with genital ulcers do not have syphilis but are treated nonetheless.

ANC screening treats approximately 4,000 women per year with an overtreatment rate of 40%, driven by the treponemal test detecting past cleared infections. KP screening through dual HIV-syphilis RDTs has a 38% overtreatment rate, while PLHIV screening has the second-highest overtreatment rate at 51%, reflecting the high cumulative syphilis exposure in these populations.

When syphilis is correctly detected and treated, the stage at which infection is identified varies across pathways (Figure 3C). GUD syndromic management predominantly detects primary-stage infections (\~87%), consistent with its reliance on visible ulcers as the trigger for care-seeking. In contrast, ANC, KP, and PLHIV screening pathways detect infections predominantly at late latent stage (66%, 54%, and 83% respectively), reflecting that the treponemal screening test identifies long-standing infections that have progressed beyond the early infectious stages. This has implications for the epidemiological impact of each pathway: GUD syndromic management, while inefficient due to overtreatment, at least targets the most infectious stage of disease, whereas the screening pathways primarily detect infections that contribute relatively little to sexual transmission.

## Impact of novel diagnostic scenarios

Under the standard of care, we estimate that total adult syphilis treatments will increase from \~64,000 per year in 2027 to 177,000 by 2040, with the overtreatment rate rising from 75% to 87% over the same period (Figure 4A). This growth is driven by population expansion and the assumed scale-up of dual HIV–syphilis RDT availability among key populations. Novel diagnostics substantially curtail this trajectory, with the magnitude depending on the use cases targeted (Figure 4A).

The overtreatment rate and number of correctly treated infections over time by scenario are shown in Figure 4B. Under single-use-case scenarios (GUD POC test or POC NT diagnostic applied to ANC, KP, or PLHIV), overtreatment declines modestly to approximately 74–75%, reflecting the limited contribution of each pathway to total treatment volume. In contrast, deploying both products across all four adult use cases reduces the overtreatment rate to 24%, a 3.4-fold reduction from SOC. The number of correctly treated infections remains essentially unchanged across scenarios (\~22,000 per year), indicating that reductions in overtreatment are achieved without reducing treatment of individuals with active infection.

Per-use-case impacts are shown in Figure 4C. The GUD POC test reduces overtreatment by 88%, replacing syndromic management with a test that identifies active infection prior to treatment. The KP and PLHIV use cases yield comparable gains, driven by large volumes of treponemal-positive but non-actively-infected individuals under dual RDT scale-up. The ANC use case contributes a smaller reduction in absolute terms due to lower treatment volumes. These differences are reflected in cumulative outcomes over 2027–2040 (Figure 4D). The GUD POC test and POC NT diagnostic applied to KP and PLHIV each avert approximately 360,000–380,000 unnecessary treatments, while ANC contributes a smaller reduction (\~30,000). The combined scenario avoids 1.16 million unnecessary treatments.

The efficiency of each diagnostic, measured as unnecessary treatments avoided per test performed, is shown in Figure 4E. The GUD POC test avoids 0.77 unnecessary treatments per test (90% CI 0.74–0.88), KP 0.72 (0.70–0.90), and PLHIV 0.76 (0.74–0.92), while ANC is lower at 0.39 (0.33–0.67).

Under the simplifying assumption that reductions in overtreatment do not affect downstream health outcomes, these efficiency estimates directly determine the cost threshold for each diagnostic (Figure 4F). A test is cost-saving whenever its price is less than the number of unnecessary treatments avoided per test multiplied by the cost of treatment. Expressed as a cost ratio (test cost / treatment cost), the break-even threshold equals the avoided-per-test fraction: 0.77 for GUD, 0.72 for KP, 0.76 for PLHIV, and 0.39 for ANC. Because all thresholds are below 1.0, diagnostics priced below the cost of a single benzathine penicillin G treatment course would be cost-saving in this framework, even before accounting for any potential health benefits from improved diagnosis.

# **Discussion**

Using an agent-based model of co-transmitting syphilis and HIV calibrated to Zimbabwe, we estimate that approximately 80% of current syphilis treatments are unnecessary, driven by the inability of existing diagnostic approaches to distinguish active infection from past treated infection or non-syphilitic presentations. Introducing point-of-care diagnostics that identify active infection substantially reduces this overtreatment down to 24% when both products are deployed across all adult use cases, while maintaining treatment of individuals with active infection. Importantly, these gains are observed under the simplifying assumption that current syndromic management and screening practices achieve high detection of active infection, and therefore do not rely on improvements in health outcomes. Under this conservative assumption, the value of improved diagnostics arises from reducing unnecessary treatment alone, which we show can yield substantial efficiency gains and, in many settings, cost savings. Together, these findings suggest that a large share of inefficiency in current syphilis management arises from diagnostic limitations, and that addressing this gap can provide meaningful benefits even without changes in treatment access or coverage.

Our findings on the potential for active infection testing to reduce overtreatment are broadly consistent with prior modeling work. Zhang et al. (24) estimated that adding a non-treponemal test to treponemal screening could reduce overtreatment by approximately 90% among pregnant women and men who have sex with men, albeit with some loss of case detection. Earlier modeling studies similarly suggested that dual treponemal/non-treponemal point-of-care tests could reduce overtreatment and be cost-saving under certain conditions (25–26). Our estimate of a 3.4-fold reduction in overtreatment under the combined scenario is of similar order of magnitude, and extends this work by embedding diagnostic strategies within a dynamic transmission model that jointly simulates syphilis and HIV, allowing us to account for stage-specific infectiousness, coinfection dynamics, and programmatic context.

However, real-world implementation has not always matched these projections. In a field evaluation in Burkina Faso, Langendorf et al. (27) found that a dual treponemal/non-treponemal screen-and-confirm assay did not reduce overtreatment compared to standard treponemal testing and was associated with substantially higher underdiagnosis. Notably, no women in the study reported a history of prior syphilis treatment, the group most likely to be overtreated with treponemal-based tests due to persistent antibody positivity. This difference in population context, along with differences in test design (combined versus sequential testing) and epidemiology, likely explains the discrepancy. Together, these findings highlight that the impact of novel diagnostics is highly context-dependent and should be evaluated within specific epidemiological and programmatic settings.

The stage distribution of detected infections across pathways has implications that complement the overtreatment findings. GUD syndromic management predominantly detects primary-stage infections, which contribute most to ongoing transmission, while screening pathways predominantly detect late latent infections with minimal transmission potential. This means that while GUD management is inefficient, the infections it does detect are epidemiologically important. Novel diagnostics that maintain detection of primary infections while reducing false positives, such as a POC GUD test, would preserve this transmission-interrupting benefit.

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting these results. First, this is not a full economic evaluation: our cost analysis is limited to a threshold-based comparison of testing costs and treatment costs, and does not account for downstream health outcomes, costs of untreated infection, or broader health system impacts. Instead, we deliberately adopt a conservative assumption that reductions in overtreatment do not affect case detection or health outcomes, allowing us to isolate the value of improved diagnostic specificity alone. Second, our model represents a single country (Zimbabwe) with specific epidemiological characteristics including moderate syphilis prevalence, a generalized HIV epidemic, and particular patterns of ANC coverage and care-seeking behavior. Our results may not generalize directly to settings with different prevalence levels, different STI etiologies of GUD, or different health system structures. Third, we did not model behavioral responses to the introduction of novel diagnostics, such as changes in care-seeking if patients become aware that testing is available, or changes in provider behavior if false-positive rates are known to be lower. Third, our model assumes a fixed sensitivity and specificity for each diagnostic test across all settings and populations; in practice, test performance may vary with sample quality, operator training, and patient population. Fourth, while we modeled HIV-syphilis coinfection dynamics, we did not include other STIs that may affect GUD etiology (such as HSV, chancroid or lymphogranuloma venereum). Fifth, the model was calibrated using data from ZIMPHIA 2015-2016, and syphilis epidemiology may have shifted in the intervening years; however, the relative comparisons between diagnostic scenarios are likely robust to moderate changes in prevalence. Finally, our model of ANC screening coverage reflects the gap between ANC attendance and actual syphilis test availability or uptake, but does not distinguish between the structural and individual-level barriers that drive this gap.

Our findings have several implications for syphilis diagnostic policy. First, the near-complete elimination of overtreatment under the combined scenario would substantially reduce benzathine penicillin consumption. Global shortages of benzathine penicillin have been a persistent concern (8–10), and reducing unnecessary use by over 84,000 treatments per year in Zimbabwe alone would meaningfully ease demand pressures and improve supply security for settings where treatment is most needed. Second, the combined deployment of both a GUD POC test and a POC NT active infection diagnostic would have a far greater impact than either alone. The GUD POC test addresses the highest-volume use case (syndromic management), while the POC NT active infection diagnostic addresses the use cases growing treatment volumes (notable KP screening as dual HIV-syphilis RDTs are deployed). 

More broadly, these findings illustrate a general principle: in settings where disease prevalence is low relative to the population screened or treated presumptively, even modest improvements in diagnostic specificity can yield large reductions in overtreatment. As syphilis screening programs expand, driven by dual HIV-syphilis RDT adoption and WHO elimination targets, the absolute volume of overtreatment will grow unless diagnostic algorithms are updated to incorporate confirmatory testing. POC confirmatory tests that can be deployed at the same visit as the initial screen, avoiding the loss to follow-up associated with laboratory-based confirmation, represent a particularly promising approach for resource-limited settings.
